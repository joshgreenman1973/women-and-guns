Black women are the fastest-growing group of new gun owners

Gun purchases by Black women surged 87% in 2021, making them the fastest-growing demographic of new gun owners. Of the 3.5 million women who became first-time owners from 2019 to 2021, 21% were Black women — a share far exceeding their proportion of the general population. The reasons cited most often: rising violent crime, social unrest, and a distrust that police would protect them.

Source: NSSF, 2021, American Journal of Preventive Medicine, 2024

Intimate partner gun homicides of women are rising, not falling

Two-thirds of all intimate partner homicides are committed with a firearm, and 77% of intimate partner firearm homicide victims are women. Among women killed with a gun who knew their killer, 61% were killed by a spouse or intimate partner.

The trend is worsening. From 2014 to 2023, intimate partner homicides of women increased 22%, driven by gun homicides, which increased 36% over the same period.

The racial disparity is stark: Black women are twice as likely to be fatally shot by a partner as white women. For Black women aged 18–34, the rate is nearly three times higher.

Source: Everytown, Violence Policy Center, CDC MMWR

Many women who straw-purchase guns are themselves victims

Reporting from the Star Tribune and The Trace has documented how women in abusive relationships are coerced into buying firearms for partners who are legally prohibited from owning them. They have clean records. Their abusers do not.

In Philadelphia, women accounted for 24% of 559 straw purchasing defendants since 2018 — despite committing less than 10% of the city's shootings. Prevention programs like Operation LIPSTICK now target outreach specifically to women about the dangers and consequences of straw purchasing.

Source: The Trace, The Philadelphia Citizen
Chapter 6

Women buy guns for protection — but the research says gun ownership makes women less safe, not more

The number one reason women buy guns is self-defense. But peer-reviewed studies consistently find that living with a handgun owner dramatically increases a woman's risk of being shot.

The promise
72% of women gun owners cite self-defense as a major reason for ownership
Women's concealed carry permits growing at 2x the rate of men's
~60,000–65,000 defensive gun uses per year (NCVS, all genders)
The research
Women living with handgun owners are 7x more likely to be shot by a partner
Living with a handgun owner increases homicide risk by 2.3x
Only 2% of violent crime victims use a gun in self-defense
Source: Stanford Medicine, 2022, Annals of Internal Medicine, AJPH/NCVS analysis, CPRC/SSRN
For every justifiable homicide by a civilian using a firearm, there are roughly 34 criminal gun homicides, 78 gun suicides, and 2 accidental gun deaths. Violence Policy Center, using FBI and CDC data
Chapter 7

A patchwork of laws attempts to keep guns from domestic abusers — but 28 states have no mandatory removal mechanism

Federal law and a landmark Supreme Court ruling have strengthened protections, but enforcement gaps leave millions of women at risk.

1996
The Lautenberg Amendment prohibits gun possession by anyone convicted of misdemeanor domestic violence — but only against a spouse, co-parent, or cohabitant.
2003
Landmark Campbell et al. study establishes that abuser gun access increases femicide risk by 500%.
2022
The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act partially closes the “boyfriend loophole” — but the restriction expires after 5 years if no new offenses occur.
2024
In United States v. Rahimi, the Supreme Court upholds (8–1) the federal ban on gun possession under DV restraining orders.

Chapter 8

Gun culture has been remade in a female image — from “mama bear” marketing to concealed carry fashion

The cultural landscape of women and guns has transformed in a single generation: 32,000-member women's shooting leagues, a concealed carry fashion industry, and two opposing maternal movements battling over the future of gun policy.

Gun ads targeting women evolved from objectification to empowerment in a decade

Pre-2008
Ads targeted exclusively at men. Women rarely shown; when present, in passive or sexualized roles.
2008–2012
Women appear in ads but still mainly sexualized — aimed at men buying guns “for her.”
2013–2015
Major shift: women depicted as competent, empowered users. “The Domestic Defender” and “The Capable Carrier” frames emerge.
2016–2020
The “Serious Student” frame dominates ~45% of ads studied. Women shown training, learning, mastering firearms.
